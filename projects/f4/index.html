The F₄ series

In these pages we present some results of calculations with trivalent graphs. The aim of the project is to reproduce the result announced in Thurston (2004) and to provide some insight into the calculation.

The starting point is the cobordism category \(C\) of trivalent graphs. This is a rigid symmetric monoidal category whose monoid of objects is $\mathbb{N}$. The object $[1]$ has a symmetric quadratic form and a compatible symmetric cubic form. Then we take free linear category over the polynomial ring $\mathbb{Q}[n]$ and impose relations. This should be thought of as the analogue in the setting of two-dimensional algebra of a finitely presented algebra in the setting of one-dimensional algebra.

The main relation we impose is the six-term relation. The object $[1]$ has a symmetric product and we write $N$ for the norm. The six-term relation is the polarisation of the identity \[ N(x^2) = N(x)^2 \] We also impose a relation for each polygon with at most four sides. The motivation is that these relations are satisfied by a cubic Jordan algebra. The result announced in Thurston (2004) is that these relations imply that the parameter \(n\) satisfies a polynomial equation of degree 9.

For $k\geqslant 0$, \( C_k\) is the free $\mathbb{Q}[n]$-module on the set of (isomorphism classes of) trivalent graphs with at most $k$ vertices. Note that a trivalent graph has an even number of vertices. By convention, \( C_0 \cong \mathbb{Q}[n] \). For $k\geqslant 0$, \( S_k\) is the free $\mathbb{Q}[n]$-module on the set of (isomorphism classes of) graphs with one four valent vertex and at most $k-2$ vertices.

The fundamental relation is the six-term relation among four-boundary trivalent graphs. Together with reduction rules for bigons, triangles, and squares, this presentation allows evaluation of any closed trivalent graph of girth at least 5.

By computer search, every closed cubic graph of girth ≥ 5 up to 16 vertices can be evaluated using this relation. However, consistency of the evaluations requires the polynomial \[ (n-26)(n-14)(n-8)(n-5)(n-2)^2\, n\,(n+1)(n+2) = 0. \]

This is Thurston’s result (2004). The nine roots correspond to known Lie algebra representations in the F₄ row of Freudenthal’s magic square.
